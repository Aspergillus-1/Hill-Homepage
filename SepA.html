Aspergillus nidulans
Protein-Protein Interactions Between SepA and PkcA

The formin SepA and the protein kinase PkcA play essential roles in both apical growth and cytokinesis in Aspergillus nidulans.   Deletion of either gene is lethal.   We have demonstrated that both proteins colocalize at sites of septation (cytokinesis) and at the hyphal apex, though a sub-population of the apical SepA population is localized separately in a structure called the Spitzenkörper (Spk), in which PkcA has not been observed.   We also demonstrate a functional relationship between the two protein, in that elevated expression of SepA compensates for deficiency in the function of PkcA.

The image below on the left demonstrates colocalization of SepA and PkcA at sites of septation (left-hand panels) and the growning hyphal apex (right-hand panels).   The image on the right below shows the inability of a PkcA-mutant strain (R78) to grow in the presence of the wall-inhibitory chemical Calcofluor White (CFW), while the wild type strain (A28) and a strain containing the PkcA plus over-expression of SepA (R1882) can tolerate the inhibitory chemical.   (The left-hand panel in the image shows all three strains growing in the absence of CFW, and the right-hand panel in the image shows growth in the presence of CFW.)

To investigate whether the colocalizing proteins are actually physically binding to each other, we have employed the Yeast-Two-Hybrid procedure along with protein truncation (selective removal of protein domains).   In the image below, the upper diagram depicts the various domains of SepA.   Below that are shown four different truncation constructs that were paired with full-length PkcA in the Y2H procedure.   Protein-protein interaction between PkcA and SepA is shown by the ability of yeast colonies to grow in the absence of the amino acid histidine, a property shown only by the two constructs having intact FH2 domains.

The gene has been cloned and shown to be an orthologue of COG4, a component of the Golgi apparatus COG (Conserved Oligomeric Golgi) vesicle tethering complex.  The swoP1 mutant allele is predicted to lack the last 55 amino acids at the C terminal.   Under the direction of Sara Gremillion (Armstrong State University) and in collaboration with Steve Harris (University of Nebraska), we have characterized the phenotype of the swoP1 mutant along with that of the related podB1 mutant in COG2. (Gremillion et al., 2014)

By overexpressing known A. nidulans COG orthologues in swoP1 (COG4-defective) or podB1 (COG2-defective) mutant backgrounds, Sara has shown genetic interactions between COG4 and COG2 (over-expression of either can complement the other's phenotype) and between COG3 and COG2.  Over-expression of COG3 complements the podB1 (COG2-defective) phenotype, but not that of swoP1.  Overexpression of the remaining COG orthologues (COG1, COG6, and COG6) is without effect on either mutation.  These results are consistent with proposed structures of the "A-lobes" of yeast and mammalian COGs.

GFP chimeras of COG2 and COG4 demonstrate localization of each protein to subcellular compartments consistent with the fungal Golgi apparatus (see below).
